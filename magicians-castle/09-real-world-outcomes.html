Visionary art

Visionary art usually defines a relatively narrow period beginning with Ernst Fuchs and flourishing into the psychedelic revolution. It often overlaps with psychedelic art. Most art in any area is drab, and stereotypical, and psychedelic and visionary art are no different. However if we are to use these terms correctly, visionary and psychedelic by definition would be highly original. They look beyond what is known and expand the psyche. As such stylistic recognisability is hard to define and is antithetical to visionary art. When we use the term in this way we can look back through time and find that all the really great art is visionary.

Sci-fi

The Science fiction genre allows for the greatest expansion of consciousness. The greatest possibility to create links. It allows complete freedom to draw on any source, embracing fantasy, the "real" world, the distant future, mythology, aliens, magic, time travel, spirituality etc.

I think what we are really talking about here though is that my paintings are figurative and that they may even have a narrative, which has been mostly absent from recent popular painting. Why not integrate the rich realm of narrative? I suppose I have gravitated towards a sci-fi picture style as it is in line with my maximal tastes. I mean, if one is trying to integrate as many symbols as possible including understandings of space that have taken place within painting history such as perspectival devices, atmosphere and the feeling of the picture being a window onto a world or a stage, then I'm not sure what other style would be left? The sci-fi diorama seems the natural outcome to me! haha...

I think some people cringe when they hear the word sci-fi, I think this may be perhaps because we are worried that we are about to experience something that may only tick the boxes of the trope, rather than pushing the envelope and introducing some original thought. I think each classification comes with its own opportunities and pitfalls, there are plenty of emotional and conceptual resonances and meandering that sci-fi landscapes may conjure up. Some of my paintings go further than others into this genre's tropes, they become almost immediately recognisable, like a 70's sci-fi novel cover. I hope these paintings will approach the viewer playfully, luring in with their public accessibility, and introducing them to this world, which can then branch outwards, linking in further concepts.

Romanticism

Romanticism is all about reclaiming the body of emotion. Arising first out of the industrial revolution as we were undergoing a materialist spiritual split. Romanticism is needed once again to seduce the viewer into an experience of chaos that is outside their consumer ideology.

Nigel Cooke and Peter Doig are good examples of this type of radical painting - one which contains a romantic spirit, and uses aesthetic effects to make non-linear arguments. After discussing examples from these artists, we will return to the death of painting and how photography, conceptualism and cinema feeds into the resurrection of painting as romantic.

"Medium" once designated the physical material that you worked with, and within the parameters of, to create. For quite some time now it has moved beyond the physical medium to the embodied medium; or to the apparatus of that medium. Painting in this expanded field, using non-traditional materials, is an embodiment of painting without using the physical medium. A comprehensive definition of "medium" will take on both the physical and embodied. It is in using physical paint that the full force of medium comes into play, and perhaps it is for this reason that painting, more so than sculpture or photography, has held onto the physical nature of its medium. Perhaps this is mostly due to its long history.

Nigel Cooke is an artist that provides a good example of a kind of painting that is New Romanticism. He provides an example of engagement with a history of the medium, in a contemporary context, along with his use of multiple levels of syntax within a pictorial whole. To paint now, in a period full of postmodern ironies, one must be fully aware of all the rejections of modernism that have taken place. When Nigel Cooke paints it is from a Metamodern standpoint, that is he tries to create something beautiful, that at the same time contains a healthy scepticism and irony. He at once acknowledges the death of painting and creates something new in the dark space that follows, in the disintegration of the pictorial space.

Nigel Cooke's self criticality is embodied by his characters that take on the artist's seemingly pointless search for meaning, they are depicted in desolate post-apocalyptic landscapes with often storm-like masses of abstract brush strokes filling the sky. Nigel Cooke describes these characters as "a generic creative figure, I wanted to embody creative thought in a certain form. Part Van Gogh and part philosopher tramp vagrant from ancient historical painting, a St Sebastian or St Jerome figure." I think if we look at St. Jerome In The Wilderness by Andrea Mantegna we can easily see St Jerome fitting into a scene of Cooke's such as To work is to play. In Mantegna's Jerome Painting, we can see a similar composition to some of Cooke's Paintings: the large rock behind Jerome takes up most of the painting and dwarves the figure, and to the sides of the rock we find keyhole like vistas onto far away landscapes. Mimosa in Valbonne is a good example of a similarly composed painting. Both Mantegna and Cooke are trying to unite multiple genres, for Mantegna the landscape and the figure painting, for Cooke abstract painting and cartoon and figures in a landscape.

If we take the traditional Jerome in the wilderness as painted throughout history, the depictions of wilderness seem to become more foreign than other paintings which may be set indoors, perhaps this is due to the highly constructed nature of an outdoor scene, which must logistically be painted inside. If we take Titian's St. Jerome In The Wilderness this construction is highly evident, and yet it does not take away from the painting but adds to it. We feel the man, the rock, the tree and the background as separate elements resting in the dim stage set of the painting surface, here we truly feel the desolation, and the abstinence of the monk that has removed himself from the world of man. In Cooke's To work is to play, we find an equally desolate constructed landscape, architectural forms reminiscent of Russian constructivist architecture and concrete jungle type contemporary public architecture. Squares and rectangles seem to drift out of this architecture and to rest ambiguously between the scene and the canvas/picture plane, perhaps these have drifted out of a Mondrian or another abstract hard edge painting. Cooke's Painting contrasts Mondrian in his irony, Cooke's constructed world full of art and artist like figures is always falling apart, it is doomed to fail from the start, Cooke knows this and yet he continues to paint anyway. Mondrian on the other hand continued his modernist paintings for many years seemingly without much change once he found his signature style. Where was Mondrian's self criticality? He believed he found the truth without question, and was brazen enough to give up on many tropes of figurative representation, thus abandoning common man, in his eyes rising up to a new plane. But where is the beauty here? And most importantly where is the human? Mondrian's Heroism is reflected in the failed socialist architecture depicted in To work is to play, the utilitarian utopian vision has descended into a ghetto for the hopeless, perhaps this is the fate that awaits the hubris of the heroic artist.

The potential of the painting medium lies in its set of coordinates, its terrain in art history. It takes attributes and areas of reference from its long history as well as modern and postmodern challenges that have changed it forever. At multiple points the challenges to painting have been so fundamental, that painting was claimed to be dead. However with the clarity of vision that comes from observing the actual effect on painting, we can see that these catalyst moments have only deepened it. in the words of Nietzsche "what does not kill me makes me stronger".

Photography killed painting; it killed the photographic part, painting as a documentary tool. This allowed painting to be reborn, as an expression of things other than documentation: leading to expressionism, symbolism, futurism and other Avant-garde movements. Similarly Duchamp with the readymade, and conceptual art that followed, killing painting again by privileging the idea over the image. Painting from a conceptual art standpoint, was seen as too traditional and beautiful. Beauty and feeling were disregarded and often rejected, and the idea was held as the groundwork from which physical material was constructed. Anti-aesthetic here rests as the default position. Conceptual art stands within postmodernism, it is one of many reactions to the naive heroism of modernism, along with installation and performance art.

After modernism and its rebounding critical postmodernism, painting has been repeatedly dismissed in art criticism. Painting is no longer a default position to hold for any educated contemporary artist. To paint now is a conceptual choice, it is anti-heroic, and it is a resurrection of spontaneity. As a conceptual choice it contains an enormous syntax of philosophical spiritual and social ideas. Along with its materiality that allows room for anti-narrative. Painting can be more radical than most conceptual art, as its nature is indeterminate, a picture cannot be expressed in words, and is thus a confounding non-lingual eminence, that sears into the eyes of our science-obsessed age. Unlike in photography and cinema the brush mark itself becomes a construction, we are confronted with an ever dissolving set of forms that move between gesture, cartoon, dream, light based representation, line based arabesque, colourful daubs, stains, and particle clouds. Here there is no ground and there is nothing to grasp, we just fall forever past possibilities of worlds we won't know. This is a body without organs, and a thousand plateaus.

"Painting can no longer just be painting. Today it is also necessarily a form of conceptual art, and as such it must be judged in relation to conceptual practices in other media, and in turn it must hold its own in this comparison." — VERWOERT

Though Paintings recent dark ages following its deaths, the hopeful romantic spirit has fled to other mediums such as photography and most notably cinema. Cinema is the most effective medium, it has the most access to the emotions through an enveloping experience, it is also time based and therefore can contain music in sync with images. As a medium closely related to photography it managed to avoid the death of figuration that painting went through. It remained as a storytelling medium that was never to forgo beauty. Through film makers such as Tarkovsky, Ingmar Bergman, Hitchcock, Kubrick and even David Lynch whose anti-narrative and multi planar films, that sit happily within a postmodern framework, still remain beautiful and moving on an emotional level. Cinema in this sense managed to retain a certain amount of romanticism. And as Painting once again regains its romantic spirit, in a nonetheless more self-conscious form, it draws from cinema as its most immediate predecessor. This is necessary because cinema doesn't have the absolute freedom of painting, the propensity for self-reflexive, process based creation. The artist begins to understand as they make, more than other mediums. As everything that the artwork will be is already there to witness, unlike the movie editing process, which always sits between shooting and viewing. Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

A great biomarker for health is our HRV. The heart naturally has a variation in the rate at which it beats, if we are healthy it will speed up on our in breath and slow down on our out breath. As we do this we move between the sympathetic autonomic nervous system on the in breath and the parasympathetic ANS on the out breath. If we are unhealthy and in a state of constant anxiety we won't have the variability in our heart rate.

There is a natural resting breath rate of about 6 breaths per minute. We can regulate our breath to this speed and if we do so we will optimise the coherence between our breathing and our HRV. If we measure our HRV in the morning after waking up we get an idea of how well our HRV and our parasympathetic ANS is performing. And if we do this everyday we can recognise trends and adjust accordingly. If we are seeing a downward trend in HRV we might be over stressing ourselves and should try to increase sleep or take a rest day from working out. If we are seeing an upwards trend we could benefit from adding some more temporary stressors such as intensity to our workouts, we would then see a temporary decrease in HRV but we would be increasing our HRV long term, which is the more important value.

HRV will alert us to overreaching days or weeks before an increasing resting heart rate or other symptoms will let us know something is wrong.

Many of the physical inversion swings mentioned in this article will increase HRV, such as deep sleeping/working out, light dark swings, etc.
